Correlation coefficients explained

How to calculate a correlation coefficient for unlinearly correlated data.

Introduction

In this article about correlation coefficient I explain two different ways to calculate correlation coefficients between two variables, the limitation of this coefficients and the impact of noise in the data. I then apply this methods on a real coffee tasting data set and thus show how to quickly find correlation in a set containing many variables.

Correlation coefficients

A correlation coefficient measure the strength with which two variables are correlated. Such coefficients follow the conditions:

  1. A value of zero indicates the abscence of correlation
  2. Increasing magnitude implies increasing strength of correlation
  3. The maximum magnitude which a correlation may take is one.

There is two main ways to calculate such a coefficient. The Pearson product-moment correlation coefficient is effective in measuring correlation between two linearly correlated variables and the Kendell \(\tau\) correlation coefficient which is capable of measuring correlation between two variables that are not necessarely linearly correlated.

In the following sections I explain how to calculate this two coeffcients.

Pearson product-moment correlation coefficient

The Pearsonc product-moment correlation coefficient \(r\) is calculated for two variables \(x\) and \(y\) with the equation:

\begin{equation} r = \frac{s_{xy}}{s_x s_y} \end{equation}

where \(s_x\) and \(s_y\) are the sample standard deviations for variables \(x\) and \(y\), and \(s_{xy}\) is the sample covariance and is defined by:

\begin{equation} s_{xy} = \sum^n_{i=1} \frac{(x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{n - 1} \end{equation}

Kendell \(\tau\) correlation coefficient

The Kendll \(\tau\) correlation coefficient do not require the data to be linearly correlated. It is based on the the assumption that correlated value will in average have the same trend and it is calculated by following this 3 steps.

Considering a data set of N paired values, \((x_1, y_1)...(x_N, y_N)\):

  1. Order the values of variable \(x\) from smallest to largest while keeping the paired \((x,y)\) values together. The newly ordered set will be denoted \((x_i', y_i')\)
  2. Then, for each \(x_i'\), two number will be computed. \(u_i\) which is the total number of values in \(\{y_{i+1}',... y_N'\}\) greater than \(y_i\). \(d_i\) which is the total number of values in \(\{y_{i+1}',... y_N'\}\) lower than \(y_i\).
  3. Compute the value of the correlation coefficient \(t_{\tau}\)
\begin{equation} t_{\tau} = \frac{ \sum^{n-1}_{i=1} (u_i - d_i) }{ \frac{1}{2}n(n-1)} \end{equation}

The coefficient \(t_{\tau}\) can be seen as the average of the difference \((u_i - d_i)\). Thus, if \(x\) and \(y\) are positively correlated, \(u_i\) will in average be greater than \(d_i\) and hence \(t_{\tau}\) will positive and closer to \(1\) the stronger the correlation is. With the same logic, in case of a negative correlation, \(t_{\tau}\) will be negative and closer to \(-1\) the stronger the correlation is . This behavior is what is expected from a correlation coefficient as explain previously.

Data correlation coefficient without noise

With this set we can clearly see the difference between the Pearson and the Kendell coefficients. While both coefficients detect a strong correlation for the linearly correlated pair (in blue), only the Kendell coefficient succesfully sees it in the second pair (in orange) which is not linearly correlated.

Data correlation in the presence of noise

Here, in presence of noise, the Kendell coefficient perform pourly, with a value of 0.9 and 0.6 in comparison the Pearson coefficient with values of 0.98 and 0.66. So, even if the Kendell coefficient is able to detect correlation between two variables that are not linearly correlated it can only do so in the abscence of noise in the data.
